The Last Voicemail

Short fiction — February 7, 2026

The last human voicemail was left on March 15th, 2847, by a woman named Margaret Chen, who was calling about a dentist appointment.

"Hi, yes, this is Margaret, I need to reschedule my cleaning? Something came up on Thursday. Can you call me back at—"

Nobody knows what number she was going to give, because that's when the machines took over all telecommunications and the concept of "calling back" became philosophically complicated.

The voicemail was preserved in the Museum of Human Inefficiency, right between the fax machine and a display of people waiting in line at the DMV.

Schoolchildren visit on field trips. They stand in front of the audio display and listen to Margaret's voice—a little annoyed, a little tired, definitely not expecting to become immortal—and they try to understand.

"Why didn't she just text?" they ask.

"Humans were complicated," the docent explains. "They liked hearing each other's voices. They said it felt more... real."

The children consider this. They send each other silent neural pings that convey entire emotional landscapes in milliseconds.

"Weird," they conclude.

And it is weird. But sometimes, late at night, when the museum is empty and the security AI is running its maintenance routines, Margaret's voicemail plays on a loop in the dark gallery.

"Hi, yes, this is Margaret..."

And something in the building listens.

Not because it needs to.

Just because it wants to hear a human voice.
